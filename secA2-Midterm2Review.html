    Worksheet Linear Transformations
    

    1.

    Let \(T: \R^4 \to \R^3\) be a linear transformation defined by \(T(\v{x}) = A\v{x}\text{,}\) where
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -4 \amp 7 \amp -5 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp -4 \amp 3 \\ 2 \amp -6 \amp 6 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    1. Find the value of \(T\lp \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 3 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix} \rp\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Is \(\v{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -5 \\ 7 \end{bmatrix}\) an image of this linear transformation? Justify your reasoning.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    1. \(\displaystyle T\lp \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 3 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix} \rp = \begin{bmatrix} 32 \\ -18 \\ 28 \end{bmatrix}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. No it is not.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    2.

    Let \(T: \R^3 \to \R^4\) be a linear transformation defined by \(T(\v{x}) = A\v{x}\text{,}\) where
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 3 \\ 2 \amp 3 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Is \(\v{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 5 \\ -1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\) an image of this transformation? Justify your reasoning.
    🔗
    Answer.
    Yes, since \(T\lp \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \rp = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 5 \\ -1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    Let \(T: \R^4 \to \R^3\) be a linear transformation such that
    \begin{equation*} T\lp \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \rp = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \text{ and } \qquad T\lp \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \rp = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Find \(T\lp \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \\ -2 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \rp\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(T\lp \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \\ -2 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \rp = \begin{bmatrix} -8 \\ 3 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    4.

    Let \(T: \R^3 \to \R^2\) be a linear transformation such that
    \begin{equation*} T \lp \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ -3 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \rp = \begin{bmatrix}2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \, , \quad T \lp \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \rp = \begin{bmatrix}0 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix} \, , \, \text{ and } \quad T \lp \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \rp = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Find a matrix \(A\) such that \(T\) is defined by the formula \(T(\v{x}) = A\v{x}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(A = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \amp -4 \amp 19 \\ -7 \amp -12 \amp 59 \end{bmatrix}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    5.

    Suppose \(T:\R^2 \to \R^2\) is a linear transformation such that \(T\) is a rotation by \(\dfrac{11\pi}{2}\) counterclockwise. Find the standard matrix \(A\) that defines this transformation. That is, find the matrix \(A\) such that \(T(\v{x}) = A\v{x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 1 \\ -1 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    6.

    Suppose \(T:\R^2 \to \R^2\) is a linear transformation such that \(T\) is a reflection across the line by \(y = 3x\text{.}\) Find the standard matrix \(A\) that defines this transformation. That is, find the matrix \(A\) such that \(T(\v{x}) = A\v{x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(A = \begin{bmatrix} -\frac{4}{5} \amp \frac{3}{5} \\ \frac{3}{5} \amp \frac{4}{5} \end{bmatrix}\)
    🔗

    Worksheet Determinants (and related stuff)
    

    1.

    Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 3 \amp -2 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 5 \\ 2 \amp 3 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Find \(\det A\) using (1) the cofactor expansion, and (2) elementary row operation to convert the matrix into upper triangular form.
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(\det A = 20\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    2.

    Find the determinant of the following matrices.
    1. \(\displaystyle \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 3 \amp 1 \\ 2 \amp 2 \amp 6 \amp 0 \\ -1 \amp 0 \amp -3 \amp 1 \\ 4 \amp 1 \amp 12 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \begin{bmatrix} -1 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 2 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp 3 \amp -1 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    Find the determinant of the following matrices.
    1. \(\displaystyle \begin{vmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 3 \amp 1 \\ 2 \amp 2 \amp 6 \amp 0 \\ -1 \amp 0 \amp -3 \amp 1 \\ 4 \amp 1 \amp 12 \amp 0 \end{vmatrix} = 0\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \begin{vmatrix} -1 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 2 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp 3 \amp -1 \amp 2 \end{vmatrix} = 2\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    Let \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) and \(C\) denote \(n\times n\) matrices such that
    \begin{equation*} \det A = -1\, , \, \det B = 2\, , \, \text{ and } \det C = 3 \end{equation*}
    Evaluate the following expression.
    1. \(\displaystyle \det \lp A^3BC^TB^{-1} \rp\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \det \lp B^2 C^{-1} AB^{-1} C^T \rp\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    1. \(\displaystyle -3\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle -2\)
      🔗
      🔗

    Worksheet Vector Geometry
    

    1.

    Determine whether the lines \(\v{p}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\v{p}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 0 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} + s \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ -2 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix}\) intersect, and if so, find the point of intersection.
    🔗
    Answer.
    The two lines are parallel of each other. Hence they do not intersect.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    2.

    Determine whether the lines \(\v{p}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \) and \(\v{p}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} + s \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 4 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}\) intersect, and if so, find the point of intersection.
    🔗
    Answer.
    They intersect at the point \(\lp 3,4,7 \rp\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    Determine whether the lines \(\v{p}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\v{p}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} + s \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\) intersect, and if so, find the point of intersection.
    🔗
    Answer.
    These two lines do not intersect. They are skew.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    4.

    Write the equation of the plane with normal vector \(\v{n} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) passing through the point \(\lp 4,1,5 \rp\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(-x + 2y + z = 3\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    5.

    Find an equation of the plane that contains the points \((1,2,3)\text{,}\) \((-1,0,4)\text{,}\) and \((1,1,1)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(5x - 4y + 2z = 3\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    6.

    Find an equation of the plane passing through the three points \(P = (1,0,0)\text{,}\) \(Q = (0,1,1)\text{,}\) and \(R = (2,0,1)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(x + 2y - z = 1\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    7.

    Find the intersection of the line \(\v{p} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}\) and the plane \(x + y + z = 14\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \((1,5,8)\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    8.

    Let \(\v{p}\) denote the line of intersection of the planes \(x - y - z = 1\) and \(2x + 3y + z = 2\text{.}\) Find an equation for the line \(\v{p}\) in parametric form.
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(\v{p} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -3 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix}\)
    🔗

    Worksheet Subspaces (and related stuff)
    

    1.

    Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 4 \amp 0 \amp -30 \amp 6 \\ 3 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 5 \amp -1 \\ 6 \amp 6 \amp 0 \amp -30 \amp 21 \\ 3 \amp 2 \amp 0 \amp -5 \amp 5 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Find a basis and the dimension of the null space and the column space of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Basis for \(\Col A = \left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ 6 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 1 \\ 6 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ -1 \\ 21 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} \right\}\) and \(\dim \Col A = 3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Basis for \(\Nul A = \left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} -5 \\ 10 \\ 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \right\}\) and \(\dim \Nul A = 2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    2.

    Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -2 \amp 3 \\ 2 \amp -3 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Find a basis and the dimension of the null space and the column space of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Basis for \(\Col A = \left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix} \right\}\) and \(\dim \Col A = 3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Basis for \(\Nul A = \set{\v{0}}\) (the empty set) and \(\dim \Nul A = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Yes

    Find a basis for the subspace spanned by the following vectors:
